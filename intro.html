INTRODUCTION + BACKGROUND

A Constructivist Culture


This past century, while prominent learning theorists

Vygotsky, Papert, and Piaget were making influential waves in the world of education and the social sciences, a subculture, one which exemplifies the very theories laid out by the aforementioned theorists, was taking shape in Southern California, emerging from the Pacific Ocean’s coastal customs, traversing paved school yard embankments, and catching flight in a drought-induced mecca of barron backyard pools. This subculture has long been regarded, at least by those paying any attention, as gritty, creative, rebellious, and resourceful; as a youthful culture that notices the potential in the seemingly mundane and embodies the do-it-yourself ethos; as an intrinsically motivated group of autodidacts, independently emulating the movements of the experts seen on the now omnipresent screen. This subculture is as nuanced as any other performing art and is just as punishing as any other high impact sport; often described as poetry in motion and proudly compared to and accompanied by jazz. There is so much more to this culture than the archetypal adolescent placing one foot on the board, one foot on the ground, and setting sail with wild abandon down Wilshire Boulevard. Architecture, art, education, engineering, and government have all been influenced in some way by said adolescent and his way of life. While some argue that participants in this culture can thrive in numerous creative endeavors, there is one such pocket where, in this author's mind, skaters do things in a unique fashion. The study I am embarking on aims to examine how the skateboarding subculture uniquely interacts with media and technology, and how learning and knowledge acquisition occurs within this domain.

My Obsession with skateboarding

began in the mid-1990’s in Orange County, California. I was maybe seven or eight years old, and was not yet aware that the skateboard I was about to pick up was going to mold my personality, guide my future, and become my motivation to learn, a common sentiment shared by so many lifers. By the time I entered high school, I did not just ride a skateboard, skateboarding was completely my identity. As my obsession grew, my grades fell. The classroom, homework, sports, parties, and dances, basically anything that has become ubiquitous with growing up in suburban United States, became distractions, obstacles that prevented me from indulging in the culture I fell in love with. School days were anxiously spent daydreaming about that afternoons session while nights, when the darkness rendered the session over, were spent ogling the cathode ray tube television set from the never ending supply of VHS tapes filled with footage of professional skaters. or scouring the websites of my favorite brands.
